Why IRAC+ Works

IRAC+ is a structural approach to legal reasoning: build a Minimum Legal Chain, diagnose contradiction on the Square (AERR), then generate research guidance and a defensible IRAC+ draft.

The problem IRAC+ solves

Traditional analysis often jumps to conclusions (illegal, liable, unconstitutional) before the underlying legal structure is made explicit. IRAC+ slows you down just enough to separate structure from outcomes.

What makes IRAC+ different

  • Structure first. Authority, roles, jurisdiction, procedure, recognition, and repair are kept distinct.
  • Completeness by design. The Minimum Legal Chain prevents missing links and hidden assumptions.
  • Diagnosis before research. The Square (AERR) maps structural failure to targeted research paths.
  • Writing that can stand up. The output is not a verdict. It is a structurally grounded draft.

The Minimum Legal Chain (1 to 8)

Every legally meaningful event can be expressed as a chain. If one link is missing or mismatched, you have a structural contradiction.

1. source of authority 2. norm 3. actor in role 4. triggering facts 5. legal action or omission 6. target 7. legal effect 8. remedy

Structural Completeness Theorem

This theorem explains why IRAC+ can always diagnose a structural problem when legal analysis fails.

Definition (Minimum Legal Chain). Any candidate legal event may be represented as an ordered structure:

⟨ source of authority, norm, actor-in-role, triggering facts, legal action or omission, target, legal effect, remedy ⟩

A chain is well-formed if each element is present, of the correct legal kind, and relationally aligned with the others. A failure of well-formedness constitutes a structural contradiction.

Theorem (Structural Completeness).
For any candidate legal event, exactly one of the following holds:

  • The Minimum Legal Chain is well-formed, and the event is structurally coherent; or
  • The chain contains at least one structural failure classifiable by the Square of Legal Contradiction (AERR).

In other words: every structurally defective legal analysis is diagnosable by at least one contradiction type in IRAC+.

Proof sketch.
Each link in the Minimum Legal Chain corresponds to a necessary condition for legal validity. The AERR typology exhaustively enumerates the ways those conditions can fail: authority may be missing or invalid, jurisdiction exceeded, procedure malformed, recognition denied, correlativity broken, or institutional repair unavailable.

Because every violation of chain well-formedness maps to at least one named contradiction type, the contradiction typology is complete with respect to the structural representation of legal events.

This is why IRAC+ does not guess outcomes. If structure fails, it can always say where and how.

What IRAC+ does not do

  • It does not decide cases or predict winners.
  • It does not replace judgment or legal research.
  • It does not loosen standards.

Instead, it makes legal judgment accountable to structure: if the structure holds, the argument can stand. If it fails, IRAC+ shows where and why.

Square of Legal Contradiction (AERR)

Authority Chain links 1 to 3
Problems of power to bind: conferral, delegation, ultra vires, capacity to act in role.
Execution Chain links 4 to 6
Problems of acting under law: jurisdiction, procedure, timing, action or omission, targeting.
Recognition Chain links 6 to 7
Problems of legal uptake: recognition of status, correlativity of rights and duties, mismatched effects.
Repair Chain link 8
Problems of institutional correction: no avenue of remedy, failed repair, repair that reproduces contradiction.

Mapping note: AERR is keyed to the chain segments above. A and E locate primary structural failures. R and R2 locate uptake and institutional response.

Contradiction types keyed to AERR and chain links

This table shows where each type most commonly sits. Some types can span multiple quadrants.

Type Square key Chain focus What it means
Type 1: conferral failure A 1 to 3 Power to bind is missing, invalid, or not properly granted to the actor in role.
Type 2: jurisdictional contradiction E 2 to 6 Action is taken without the required jurisdiction, competence, venue, or scope.
Type 3: role contradiction A 3 (or 7) The actor lacks the legal capacity for the role, or role constraints conflict with what is claimed.
Type 4: procedural contradiction E 2 to 5 Required process is skipped, malformed, or contradictory, such as notice, hearing, warrant, or due steps.
Type 5: temporal contradiction E 2 and 4 Timing conditions fail, such as retroactivity, lapse, statute of limitations, or premature enforcement.
Type 6: recognition failure R 6 and 7 A legally relevant status, standing, entitlement, or classification is not recognized when it should be.
Type 7: correlativity contradiction R 7 Rights and duties do not match, such as a duty without a bearer, or a right with no correlative obligation.
Type 8: repair failure R2 8 The system has no effective remedy path, or institutional repair is structurally unavailable.
Type 9: recognition repair failure R2R 6 to 8 Even after a repair attempt, the institution still fails to recognize the relevant status or entitlement.
Type 10: procedural repair failure R2E 5 to 8 Repair exists in name, but the process for repair is itself defective or nonfunctional.
Type 11: jurisdictional repair failure R2E 6 to 8 The forum for repair lacks jurisdiction or authority to grant the needed correction.
Type 12: compound contradiction AERR2 multiple More than one primary contradiction type is present and they interact across chain segments.
Type 13: institutionalized contradiction R2R system-wide A contradiction persists as a stable feature of institutional operation, often producing recurring contradiction debt.
